Ida Tarbell · 1857–1944
Journalist & Historian

Ida Tarbell grew up in Pennsylvania's oil country. Her father, an oil producer, was nearly destroyed financially by John D. Rockefeller's Standard Oil Company. That experience shaped her life's mission.

In 1904, after years of meticulous research, she published The History of Standard Oil — a 19-part series in McClure's Magazine. She interviewed workers, gathered documents, and traced how Standard Oil crushed competition through secret railroad deals and price manipulation.

Her work led directly to the Supreme Court breaking up Standard Oil in 1911 — one of the most significant antitrust decisions in American history.

Method

Document research, interviews, and statistical analysis over 5 years

Target

Standard Oil & John D. Rockefeller — the world's richest man

Result

Supreme Court orders Standard Oil dissolved (1911)

Jacob Riis · 1849–1914
Journalist & Photographer

Jacob Riis immigrated from Denmark and experienced homelessness firsthand in New York City. He worked as a police reporter, spending years in the worst neighborhoods of Manhattan's Lower East Side.

In 1890, he published How the Other Half Lives, combining shocking photographs with firsthand accounts of life in tenement housing — cramped, dark apartments where immigrant families of 10 shared single rooms.

His photos of children sleeping in alleys and families in airless rooms shocked middle-class Americans. Theodore Roosevelt, then NYC police commissioner, called it "an enlightenment and an inspiration."

Method

Photography with magnesium flash powder — an early camera flash

Target

Tenement housing conditions; landlord exploitation of immigrants

Result

NYC passed new housing laws; unsafe buildings demolished

Upton Sinclair · 1878–1968
Novelist & Activist

Upton Sinclair spent seven weeks working undercover in Chicago's meatpacking plants in 1904. What he found was horrifying: diseased animals processed for food, workers injured and ignored, and unsanitary conditions throughout.

He published his findings as a novel, The Jungle (1906), hoping to create sympathy for immigrant workers. Instead the public focused entirely on food safety. As Sinclair famously said: "I aimed at the public's heart and by accident hit its stomach."

Within months of publication, Congress passed the Pure Food and Drug Act and the Meat Inspection Act of 1906.

Method

Undercover investigation embedded with workers for 7 weeks

Target

Meatpacking industry — unsafe conditions for workers AND consumers

Result

Pure Food & Drug Act (1906) — foundation of today's FDA

Case File #2

Read the Evidence

Historians don't just take other people's word for it — they read the original sources. Read carefully, then answer as a historical detective.

Source A — Upton Sinclair, The Jungle, 1906

Primary Source · Novel Based on Firsthand Investigation
"There was never the least attention paid to what was cut up for sausage… there would be meat that had tumbled out on the floor, in the dirt and sawdust, where the workers had tramped and spit uncounted billions of consumption germs. There would be meat stored in great piles in rooms; and the water from leaky roofs would drip over it, and thousands of rats would race about on it."

Source B — Ida Tarbell, The History of Standard Oil, 1904

Adapted from Primary Source · McClure's Magazine
"Mr. Rockefeller was more troubled by a competitor who made cheaper oil than by one who made better oil… It was not enough that Standard Oil got rebates — special lower shipping rates — from the railroads. It also demanded that the railroads pay it a portion of what competitors paid for shipping. The competitor was not only deprived of his equal rights, but his payments went to build up the very power that was destroying him."

Source C — Jacob Riis, How the Other Half Lives, 1890

Primary Source · Photojournalism Book
"In a room not thirteen feet either way slept twelve men and women, two or three in bunks set in a sort of alcove, the rest on the floor. A kerosene lamp burned dimly in the fearful atmosphere, probably to guide other and later arrivals to their 'beds,' for it was only just past midnight. A baby's faint wail came from an adjoining hall-room."

Case File #3

Measuring the Impact

Good journalism should change things. But did it? Rate each muckraker's impact using the evidence you've read.

🎯 Historian's Note: Impact isn't just about immediate change. Think: Did it change laws? Did it change thinking? Did the changes last? Did it help the right people?
⚖️
Ida Tarbell's Impact

Standard Oil was broken into 34 smaller companies in 1911. But those companies eventually became ExxonMobil, Chevron, and other giants we know today.

🏚️
Jacob Riis's Impact

NYC passed new tenement laws and demolished some dangerous buildings. But immigrant neighborhoods remained overcrowded for decades. By 1910, over 1 million people still lived in Manhattan tenements.

🥩
Upton Sinclair's Impact

The Pure Food & Drug Act (1906) directly resulted from public outrage over The Jungle. But Sinclair's real goal — improving workers' conditions — was largely ignored by Congress.
